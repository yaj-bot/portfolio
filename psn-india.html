PSNDeal

A deal tracker and backlog manager for PlayStation gamers. I got tired of missing discounts and built something to fix that.

The problem

PlayStation discounts are unpredictable and short. If you are not watching the right places at the right time, you find out a game had a discount after it is already back to full price. The tools that exist are patchy, region-inconsistent, and require you to wade through noise to find deals that are actually good.

Gamers across every store have been making do with Reddit threads and spreadsheets. I was one of them. When a game dropped from ₹4,000 to ₹800 for seventy-two hours, you either knew or you did not. I wanted something that watched for you.

My role

I built this alone, with AI assistance, from scratch. That meant doing every part of it: reverse-engineering Sony's storefront API, designing the data model, writing the scraper that runs daily, and building the frontend. There was no team to hand off to. Every decision about what mattered and what did not was mine.

It launched on 30 June 2026.

What I built

The scraper pulls pricing data for the PSN catalogue across regions every day and stores it. That history is what makes everything else possible: the site can tell you not just what a game costs today, but whether that price is actually a good deal relative to where it has been.

The deal scoring is weighted by discount percentage, user rating, and rating count together. A 90% off game with three reviews scores lower than a 60% off game that thousands of people have rated highly. That weighting is what separates the top deals from just the cheapest things.

Alongside deals, there is a wishlist and a backlog manager. You add games you want to buy, and the site alerts you when they drop. The backlog lets you track what you own and where you are with it. Both are the things I actually wanted when I was doing this in a spreadsheet.

Early traction

The site launched on 30 June 2026 with no marketing. Distribution at launch was a post on r/PlayStationIndia and a few gaming Discord servers. Both drove traffic immediately, which confirmed the problem was real and the audience reachable without a budget.

Next: posts across r/PS5, r/patientgamers, and r/GameDeals; outreach to gaming YouTubers and newsletter writers who cover deals; and a presence on X where deal alerts tend to spread fast. The goal is to let the product do the talking and build distribution through people who already have the audience.

What I learned

The hardest part was not the code. It was deciding what the site was actually for. A deal tracker and a backlog manager are two different tools with two different reasons to return. I kept both because I use both, but the tension between them is something I still think about as it grows.

Building without a team also means every bad decision is entirely yours. That is clarifying in a way that working inside a large product org is not. You find out quickly what actually matters to users because you are watching all the data yourself and there is nobody else to blame when something is confusing.
